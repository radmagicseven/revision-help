AQA GCSE Biology: Essential Food Tests

A clear guide to the required practicals for identifying biological molecules.

Food Sample Preparation

Before conducting any food test, prepare your sample. This ensures the reagents can mix effectively with the food substance.

Solid Food Samples

Crush the solid food using a pestle and mortar, then mix thoroughly with distilled water to create a solution.

Liquid Food Samples

No preparation is needed; you can use the liquid directly for testing.

1

Testing for Starch

The Iodine test is used to detect the presence of starch, a complex carbohydrate.

Reagent: Iodine solution (originally yellow-brown)

Procedure: Add a few drops of iodine solution directly to your food sample.

Expected Results:

Negative
Remains yellow-brown
Positive
Turns blue-black
2

Testing for Reducing Sugars

The Benedict's test identifies simple sugars like glucose and maltose. Heat is required for this reaction.

The colour change from blue indicates the presence of reducing sugars. A brick-red precipitate signifies a high concentration.

3

Testing for Proteins

The Biuret test is used to detect proteins by identifying peptide bonds. This test usually involves two solutions.

Reagents: Biuret Reagent (Potassium Hydroxide/Sodium Hydroxide and Copper(II) Sulfate solution)

Procedure:

  1. Add an equal volume of potassium hydroxide (or sodium hydroxide) solution to the food sample and shake.
  2. Then, add a few drops of dilute copper(II) sulfate solution and shake gently.

Expected Results:

Negative
Remains blue
Positive
Turns lilac/purple
4

Testing for Fats & Lipids

The Emulsion Test detects the presence of fats and oils (lipids) in a sample.

Emulsion Test Procedure

  1. Add about 2 cm of ethanol to the food sample and shake thoroughly (to dissolve any lipids).
  2. Carefully pour this ethanol solution into a test tube containing about 2 cm of distilled water.

Negative

Solution remains clear

Positive

A cloudy white emulsion forms

AQA GCSE Biology: Enzymes in Digestion

A visual guide to the key digestive enzymes, their substrates, and where they act in the body.

Understanding Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts, which means they speed up chemical reactions in living organisms without being used up themselves. In digestion, enzymes are crucial for breaking down large, insoluble food molecules into smaller, soluble molecules that can be absorbed into the bloodstream. Each enzyme is highly specific, only acting on a particular substrate, like a lock and key.

Amylase: Breaking Down Carbohydrates

Amylase is a carbohydrase, meaning it is responsible for breaking down carbohydrates. Specifically, it targets starch, a complex carbohydrate, and breaks it down into simpler sugars, primarily maltose.

Starch Maltose

Where it's Found:

  • Salivary glands (in the mouth)
  • Pancreas
  • Small intestine wall

Protease: Breaking Down Proteins

Proteases are enzymes that specialise in breaking down proteins. These large molecules are digested into their smaller building blocks, amino acids, which are then ready for absorption. Examples include pepsin and trypsin.

Proteins Amino Acids

Where it's Found:

  • Stomach (as pepsin)
  • Pancreas
  • Small intestine wall

Lipase: Breaking Down Lipids

Lipase is the enzyme responsible for the digestion of lipids (fats and oils). It breaks these large molecules down into fatty acids and glycerol, which are both small enough to be absorbed into the bloodstream.

Lipids Fatty Acids + Glycerol

Where it's Found:

  • Pancreas
  • Small intestine wall

Summary of Digestion

These three types of enzymes carbohydrases (like amylase), proteases, and lipases work together in different parts of the digestive system to ensure that all major food groups are broken down into absorbable forms. This process is essential for providing the body with the energy and building blocks it needed. Remember, enzymes are biological catalysts, specific to their substrates, and function best under optimum conditions.

Key Takeaway:

Enzymes are essential for efficient chemical digestion throughout the body.
